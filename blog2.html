Fast.ai: Lesson 1

Some lessons for people with a research background (like me)

"What do you mean I apply the method first and then try to understand it?"

- Me, in my head, after getting challenged out of my "literature first, application second" comfort zone

Welcome back! I want to share some of my experiences with the first couple of days of fast.ai's Practical Deep Learning for Coders, v3 program. I found the first video lecture very interesting, but at first also a little bit intimidating. Why? Because it follows a top-down approach. That means, that you get a fully funtional code example, where you straight away train a neural network to do some image classification. All good and well, but coming from a research background, I am so used to spend hours and days on theoretical work before I go over to actually do some practical work, so I felt like this was out of my comfort zone. Doing before understanding how it works?

It took a day until I gave in and decided to do as asked. I followed along with the examples, I got my neural network to classify breeds of cats and dogs based on pictures, and all this, as Jeremy Howard says, with state of the art accuracy. But what next? In my day to day work as a researcher, I am used to finding novel things (otherwise it is hard to get scientific paper published). And this is when I realised that I had forgotten how to focus mainly on the fun aspect. There is no pressure whatsoever to create a google brain after one lesson of fast.ai! I can do whatever I want. So what did I do?

I am in Finland at the moment, working on a research project. So why not try with something simple: a classifier that can discriminate between elks and reindeer pictures? Kind of like the Finnish version of Cats versus Dogs. And you know what? I created a model with 98% accuracy trained on 60 pictures of each reindeer and elks. Obviously there were some challenges, but as soon as I allowed myself to just apply first and understand second, it braught the fun back. All it took was a simple idea. So here are the main challenges I came across:

  • It is an image classifier. Guess what, you need images!
  • How do I get enough images of different elks and reindeer to train a model?
    • (I could spend a weekend hunting down reindeer and take photos of as many as possible?)
  • How many images do I need?
It is one thing to follow along the training of a neural network with a pre-defined image data set, but another to find your own data set. So there is obviously still some research needed, but the basic structure of the training, as provided by fast.ai still stands. After a little bit of help from Mr Google, I found that there is a python function, that allows you to specify search terms for google images and download it into folders on your computer! Amazing. That solves a lot of image classification problems. Obvioulsy one needs to be very careful with the copyrights of the images and it is not recommended to publish any of the images publicly, but it is awesome for training your model.

I will link my static jupyter notebook further down, so you can have a look at exactly what I did, but I want to just briefly show how to use the python function to access google images. First, type the following into your jupyter notebook and run it (Shift + Return):

!pip install google_images_download
Once this is installed, you have to import it:
from google_images_download import google_images_download
After the library is installed and the function is loaded, you need to set up the path (PATH in the below function) to your project and simply paste:
response = google_images_download.googleimagesdownload()

arguments = {"keywords":"Elk,Reindeer", "limit":100, "print_urls":False, "output_directory":f'{PATH}train', "chromedriver":"/usr/bin/chromedriver"}

paths = response.download(arguments)
Et voila, this function will download 100 pictures per specified category into a folder with the same name as the category, so you have a folder structure, perfectly set up for a labelled image classification.

Here you can find the jupyter notebook for this little project. I built the image recognition convolutional neural network based on resnet34. Until next time, your
